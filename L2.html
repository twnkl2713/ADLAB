How Prisons and Temp Agencies Exploit the Most Vulnerable Workers

By Mansa Musa

December 1, 2025

The Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery in 1865, but included a critical exception that has been systematically exploited for over 150 years. The amendment states that slavery and involuntary servitude are prohibited "except as a punishment for crime whereof the party shall have been duly convicted." This constitutional loophole has created a massive system of forced labor in American prisons.

Today, nearly 800,000 incarcerated individuals work in state and federal prisons across America, stripped of basic labor protections that all other workers enjoy. These workers earn between 13 cents and 52 cents per hour on average, producing at least $2 billion in goods and $9 billion worth of prison maintenance services annually. Seven states—Alabama, Arkansas, Florida, Georgia, Mississippi, South Carolina, and Texas—pay nothing at all for the vast majority of prison work.

The work is rarely voluntary. Incarcerated workers report that refusing work assignments can result in severe punishment including solitary confinement, loss of family visitation rights, and denial of parole consideration. Sixty-four percent of incarcerated workers say they worry about their safety on the job, yet they have no choice but to continue working under dangerous conditions.

Workplace safety and labor laws explicitly exempt prison laborers from protections that virtually all other workers enjoy. They fight wildfires for pennies an hour, work with toxic chemicals without proper protective equipment, and operate dangerous machinery without adequate training. During the COVID-19 pandemic, incarcerated workers were forced to take on high-risk frontline health roles, filling hand sanitizer bottles and working in medical facilities, while being denied early access to vaccines in 16 states.

A Legacy of Slavery

The exploitation is not colorblind. These systems disproportionately harm Black communities and are directly rooted in the legacy of slavery. After the Civil War, Southern states implemented Black Codes that criminalized mundane activities of formerly enslaved people, creating a new form of forced labor through the convict leasing system.

Today, the South incarcerates people—primarily Black men—at the highest rates in the world and is more likely than other regions to force incarcerated people to work for nothing. Black workers without college degrees are overrepresented in temp agency positions and more than twice as likely to be paid poverty wages compared to workers in all industries.

Temp agencies serving these communities have been documented engaging in racially discriminatory hiring practices, using code words to signal preferences for or against workers of certain races. Some agencies conduct drug tests and background checks selectively based on race.

Fighting Back

Katherine Passley, co-executive director of Beyond the Bars and named 2025 Labor Organizer of the Year by In These Times magazine, has been fighting to expose and dismantle these systems. Her organization works to connect the prison abolition movement with the labor movement, fighting for fair wages and dignity for formerly incarcerated workers.

"When we talk about abolition, we need to talk about labor," Passley explains. The exploitation of vulnerable workers through both prison labor and temp agencies serves the same purpose: maintaining a subordinate class of disproportionately Black workers whose desperation employers can exploit to maximize profits while undermining wages and working conditions for all workers.

The impact extends beyond those directly affected. Prison labor has historically been used to undermine unions and drive down wages across entire industries. By creating a permanent underclass of workers without labor protections, these systems depress wages and working conditions for everyone, making it harder for all workers to demand fair treatment and decent pay.
